Inside and Outside, How TBBT brings inspiration to Knowledge Management

Created2023-04-06|Updated2023-04-06
|Post View:

The Big Bang Theory （hereinafter to be referred to as TBBT）is one of America’s most popular sitcoms. It tells a story about a group of highly intelligent, eccentric but lovable scientist geeks and their “normal” blonde beauty neighbor. The sitcom ran for twelve seasons and brought joy to countless people, including me. Today, as a knowledge management student, I am rethinking the plot and details of the show, and I find that it can bring some inspiration to knowledge management. In the following parts, I will briefly talk about some KM practices that I found in TBBT.

Inside: KM practice in TBBT

1. Diversity

DEI[JB] tells us that diversity is very important in organisations. The composition of TBBT’s main characteristics is a good representation of Diversity. Sheldon is a theoretical physicist, Leonard is an experimental physicist, Howard has an Engineering master’s degree, Rajesh is an astrophysicist, Bernadette is a microbiologist, Amy is a neurobiologist, and Penny is a “normal” person with rich life experience. The main protagonists come from different majors, different states, different countries, and different religious beliefs. As they discuss the issues, we get to see these PhDs (and one MA) analyse the issues in multiple dimensions from different disciplinary perspectives, and we get to see how Penny surprises us with new perspectives when the geniuses are at a loss (Sheldon comes up with new ways of thinking about string theory in his conversations with her). If we think of these protagonists as an organisation, it is certainly a highly diversity organisation. And their multi-dimensional analysis and theoretical additions to the same problem are exactly what the organisation needs in its knowledge management.

From the perspective of diversity, Penny and Sheldon are two extremes, one academic genius with multiple Ph. D.s in his teens but know nothing about social life and one sophistication who has only attended community college but has been in society for many years. They didn’t understand each other at first, but in the end, they became great friends. The growth of Penny and Sheldon in TBBT is, in my opinion, a perfect example of the importance of diversity in personal growth. Penny and Sheldon learn from each other what they have never been exposed to but what the other is very good at. Sheldon learns from his interaction with Penny how to behave in the world and the rules of social interaction, becoming more humane. （just like you will learn tacit knowledge from the socialization process）. This inspires me of interacting and communicating with more people that different from me. Diversity can widen one’s horizon and knowledge sharing happens when you communicate with someone who knows many things you don’t know.

2. Lunch & Learn

Lunch and learn is a good KM practice. And in TBBT you can also find they do similar activities. You can often see the main characters getting together for dinner. In these scenes, they not only exchange anecdotes about their lives but also come up with some topics and have a dinner conversation. In S09E23, you can see Sheldon and Penny use their breakfast time to expand their respective knowledge base. And the benefit of this activity is just as Sheldon said:

“The mind received nourishment as well as the stomach”

I think this point shows exactly what lunch and learn wants to achieve. For organisations, Lunch and learn is a good way to do knowledge sharing and develop a knowledge culture within the organisation. Moreover, just like how characters show in the TBBT, Lunch and learn may also increase a sense of belonging for employees, because for me, it is also a good way to let employees know each other better.

3. Sheldon’s Simplification of packing tasks

There is a very interesting humorous slice in TBBT. When Leonard asked what Sheldon is doing before their trip, Sheldon give this answer:

“I am simplifying the task of packing for our trip. See, by attaching rfid tags to my clothing, it will enable my laptop to read and identify the items with this wand. I will then cross-reference them against destination. Anticipated activity spectrum, weather conditions duration of trip, Et cetera.”

This may sound like a stupid solution for trip preparation, but I think it can give us some inspiration on Knowledge codification and Knowledge system. What Sheldon actually is a good practice of knowledge system built.

He codifies and stores the information attached to the clothes (thickness, colour, etc.) and creates a retrieval system for later finding the solutions he needs according to the specific travel situation. Projecting this situation into the organisation’s knowledge management is equivalent to the creation of a knowledge system. It is essential for organisations to codify and store knowledge assets in a suitable way in the knowledge base and to provide a recognisable and quick retrieval of each piece of information so that employees can find the information they need as quickly as possible.

Outside: A good tool for Knowledge Sharing

Strictly speaking, TBBT cannot be considered knowledge sharing content, but its contribution to the spread of science is considerable. I learned about Schrodinger’s cat, the Doppler effect, and even became curious about string theory and dark matter (although I still cannot understand these two things). TBBT has made “boring” physics more interesting for many people and even drove some audiences to start their careers in physics research.

In the UK, between 2009 and 2011, when TBBT began broadcasting, the number of students choosing physics at A-level rose by 20% and physics was in the top 10 most popular subjects for the first time. The number of students taking physics-related subjects at university increased by 17%. TBBT, Brian Cox, and the Large Hadron Collider have become socially significant pop culture icons. Sir Peter Knight, a professor at the British Institute of Physics, admits that physics benefits from this geek-chic image (LIZI,2019).

TBBT is not popular content about teaching physics, but it did something even more remarkable, making physics, science cool. When the protagonist talks about physics and science in a humorous way, incorporating science into the tiny things in life, we find that ‘knowledge’ becomes less overwhelming.

KM process starts with knowledge identification but from a personal perspective, one thing may be more important, that is why I want to learn such knowledge. I describe it as Knowledge interest.

For the knowledge that may not be useful in the short term such as Schrödinger’s Cat, interest may be the key reason why people watch some knowledge sharing contents. TBBT arise the audience’s interest in some science topics. Rashel Lif (2016) found that many viewers with a non-science background would actively search for science-related knowledge mentioned in the show after watching it. Some have subscribed to science videos on YouTube, and some will be more willing to engage in public speeches, thus, begin their own KM process. And for organisation, I think interests is also an important issue that needs to be thought about. Because if you want your employees to sacrifice their lunch time or private time to learn something from other departments or something that seems not to be instantly beneficial for their work, you need to give them some explanation or you need to let them feel that they want to learn that.

While watching TBBT, I enjoy science a lot. After 12 years of journey with it, I feel that I grew up with the main characters. It changed my mind to the psychics, to the science, to the scientist. And now from the KM perspective, I found it equally revealing. This article is a very personal view, but I hope it can give you a little inspiration to see TBBT from a different perspective.
